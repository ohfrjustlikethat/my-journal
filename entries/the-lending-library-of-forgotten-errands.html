The Lending Library of Forgotten Errands

In a town where nothing is ever quite finished, there is a library that lends out the errands you abandoned. Due dates apply.

fiction whimsy memory

In the town of Amberish, where the fog keeps its own hours and the clocks are considered advisory, there is a library that lends not books but errands — specifically, the ones you meant to run and never did.

You will not find it by looking. It is on the street you always mean to explore and never turn down. The sign says LENDING LIBRARY OF FORGOTTEN ERRANDS, and beneath, in smaller letters: est. the day after you meant to.

The Collection

Inside, it is exactly as quiet as you fear. The stacks run back further than the building should allow. Each errand is kept in a slim grey box, filed by the person who abandoned it, cross-referenced by the year and by the excuse.

The largest section by far is Phone Calls, Unmade. The librarian says people are forever checking those out and returning them unrun, the box a little warmer, the excuse slightly better rehearsed.

You may borrow back the phone call you never made. The apology you drafted in the shower and swallowed at the door. The letter three-quarters written to the friend who moved away, still folded at the crease where you stopped believing you'd send it. The plant you forgot to water, kept here in perfect suspended almost-death, waiting.

There is a whole reading room of things you were going to look up. It hums faintly, like a fridge, with the sound of unsatisfied curiosity.

The Terms

The librarian is a patient woman with ink on the side of her hand, which is how you can tell she still writes letters she means to send. She will find your file without being told your name. Everyone's file is thicker than they expect.

The terms are gentle. There are no fines, exactly. But every errand you borrow must eventually be returned — and an errand can only be returned in one of two conditions: completed, or confessed.

You may keep the errand as long as you like. You simply may not keep it forever and keep pretending you will run it.

Most people, it turns out, choose to confess. It is easier than it sounds and harder than it looks. You take the grey box to the returns desk and you say, out loud, to a woman with ink on her hand: I was never going to make that call. I know that now. And she nods, and stamps the box, and files it under Released, which is the only section in the building that gets any lighter.

I have been three times. I have completed one errand, confessed two, and quietly re-shelved a fourth I wasn't ready for. The librarian pretended not to notice, which is her greatest kindness.

The Fine

I said there were no fines. That was not quite true. There is exactly one, and it is levied automatically, without malice, on any errand kept too long unfaced. The fine is this: the errand becomes a regret, and regrets do not live in the lending library. They move into you, and they do not pay rent, and they rearrange the furniture.

So if you ever turn down the street you always mean to explore, and the fog parts, and the small sign is lit — go in. Ask for your file. It's thicker than you think, and lighter than you fear, and the woman at the desk has been keeping the light on your smallest unfinished kindness for longer than you've been avoiding it.

Return something. Anything. Completed or confessed. Then step back out into the advisory hour, an errand lighter, and let the fog close behind you like a book you've finally finished reading.
