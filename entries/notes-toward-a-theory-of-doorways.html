Notes Toward a Theory of Doorways

On the small amnesia of walking into a room — and why I've started to think the threshold was built to cause it.

notebook thresholds architecture

You have felt it. You cross into the kitchen with a clear and urgent purpose, and somewhere on the sill the purpose evaporates, leaving you standing in the good light holding nothing. The doorway did that. I have started to think it was designed to.

Psychologists have a bloodless name for it — the location-updating effect. Cross a boundary and the mind files the old room away to make space for the new one, and your errand, poor errand, gets filed with it. The theory says the threshold is an event boundary: the brain chapters your day at the door.

The tidy experiment: people forget more walking through a doorway into an identical room than walking the same distance across one large room. The door does it, not the steps.

Fine. But the notebook is where I get to be unscientific, so: what if we knew this, long before we could measure it? What if the doorway is not a design flaw the brain suffers but a piece of architecture the brain invented, and then we built it into houses to match?

Consider how many thresholds are ceremonial. We carry brides over them. We nail things to them, hang beads across them, leave shoes beside them. We say I couldn't set foot in that house. No culture treats a doorway as merely the absence of wall.

A threshold is the one part of a house whose entire function is to be passed through and, in passing, to change you slightly.

So here is the entry, unfinished on purpose, the way the best notebook entries are: perhaps we built doorways precisely for the forgetting. A place to set down what you were carrying before you enter the next room of your life. Every arch a small mercy. Every lintel a line drawn under the sentence before.

To test later: do open-plan houses make for more relentless minds? No doors, no chapters, no place to put the errand down.

I went to get a glass of water twenty minutes ago. I came back with this instead. I'm choosing to believe the doorway knew which one I needed.
