Salt

A very short story about the sea, and the arithmetic of what it takes back.

fiction sea grief

My grandmother measured the tide not in feet but in people. The sea gave back the fishing boats, mostly. It gave back nets, and one gold ring, and — once, in the winter I was seven — a whole upright piano, waterlogged and a semitone flat, which four men carried up the shingle while she watched with her arms folded, unimpressed.

It did not give back my grandfather.

She never said his name to the water. She said it only to the bread, quietly, the way you'd tell a secret to something that couldn't repeat it.

She salted everything after that. Salt in the bread, which was ordinary. Salt in her tea, which was not. Salt thrown over the left shoulder, salt in a dish on the windowsill facing the harbour, salt worked into the rims of her eyes on the mornings she thought no one was up to see it.

I asked her once why. She was very old by then and I was not much younger than she'd been in the winter of the piano.

“So the sea knows,” she said, “that I already have all the salt I need. It can keep its bargains.”

She died on a flat calm day, the kind that gives nothing back and asks for nothing. We scattered her where the shingle goes to sand. I brought a twist of salt in my coat pocket and, at the last moment, could not bring myself to throw it. I ate it instead, a few grains on the tongue, bitter and enormous, and stood there being the arithmetic she'd taught me: one taken, one returned, the tide indifferent to the difference and going out, and out, and out.
